## History

This section chronicles the historical evolution of sequence modeling leading to the Transformer architecture, emphasizing key milestones and contextual details. Early work in the 1990s established foundational techniques. Specifically, in 1990, the Elman network, proposed by Jeffrey Elman, introduced recurrent neural networks (RNNs) for sequence processing but suffered from the vanishing-gradient problem, limiting long-sequence capabilities. This limitation persisted until 1995 when Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM), developed by Sepp Hochreiter and Jürgen Schmidhuber, addressed the issue with multiplicative units, enabling efficient learning of long-term dependencies. However, LSTM retained sequential processing, which constrained parallelization. For instance, a 380M-parameter machine translation model used LSTMs for encoding and decoding, but the fixed-size output vector caused information loss, as evidenced by the observation that reversing input sentences improved translation quality. Similarly, a 130M-parameter model used gated recurrent units (GRUs) instead of LSTMs, though research showed GRUs were comparable to LSTMs for sequence-to-sequence tasks.

In the 2010s, sequence-to-sequence (seq2seq) models emerged for machine translation. The RNN search model introduced attention mechanisms in 2014–2016, allowing the decoder to "emulate searching through a source sentence," thereby mitigating the fixed-size output bottleneck. Google Neural Machine Translation (GNMT) in 2016 replaced statistical methods with an 8-layer bidirectional LSTM seq2seq architecture, taking nine months to develop and outperforming the previous approach, which had taken ten years. Crucially, in 2016, the "Attention is All You Need" hypothesis, formulated by Vaswani et al. at Google, proposed self-attention as a key component, directly paving the way for the Transformer's revolutionary 2017 introduction. This work, rooted in the core application domain of machine translation, overcame limitations of RNNs and LSTMs by enabling parallel processing and efficient long-distance dependency handling.
